Gemini Constellation

Best seen in February around 9 p.m, Gemini is one of the easiest zodiac constellations to find. One method is just simply locating the two of its brightest stars, Castor and Pollux, with help from the Big Dipper. In finding Castor and Pollux, trace an imaginary line diagonally from the handle of the Big Dipper towards the opposite side of it’s bowl. Continuing that line, you should see Pollux and Castor above it. These two stars represent each of the “twins” heads in the Gemini constellation.
In Egyptian astrology, the constellation was identified with twin goats, while Arabian astrology said it was twin peacocks. The Western world tends to identify these twins as Castor and Pollux from Greek mythology (space.com).
For a more experienced star gazer, use the information below in finding the constellation:
  • Right ascension: 7 hours
  • Declination: 20 degrees
  • Visible between latitudes 90 degrees and minus 60 degrees
