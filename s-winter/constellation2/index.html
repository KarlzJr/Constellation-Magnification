Taurus Constellation

Best seen in January around 9 p.m, Taurus is one of the easiest zodiac constellations to find in the night sky. All that is needed is finding Orions’s belt or the Pleiades. Orion’s belt is made up of three stars in a straight line. Once found, extend an imaginary straight line from the belt that continues to Aldebaran, a bright star in the Taurus constellation. If Aldebaran can’t be seen, just simply look for the Pleiades, a group of seven stars closely bunched together. In between the Pleiades and Orion’s belt is Taurus, which is shaped like a Y with a hook.
In Greek mythology, when Zeus fell in love with the Phoenician Princess Europa, he transformed himself into a white bull with golden horns named Taurus and carried Europa away to Crete (space.com).
For a more experienced star gazer, use the information below in finding the constellation:
  • Right ascension: 4 hours
  • Declination: 15 degrees
  • Visible between latitudes 90 degrees and minus 65 degrees
