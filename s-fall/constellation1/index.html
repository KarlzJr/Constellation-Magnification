Pisces Constellation

Best seen between Nov. 6 and Nov. 9 at 9 p.m, Pisces is one of the better-known constellations in the night sky. One method that is used to find Pisces starts with finding the ecliptic line. During sunset, start by looking west towards the sunset and finding a bright red dot above the sun, which is Venus. The Moon should be rising in the opposite direction, and from there you trace or imagine a linear path from Venus to the Moon. If two hours after sunset, look east for a little red dot, which is Mars, and trace the linear path from Mars to the Moon. Then, look northeast of Aquarius to locate Pisces. The shape of Pisces resembles that of a big V shape which is one of the largest formations in the sky and a group of five stars that form an irregularly shaped pentagon, which resembles the head of the west fish.
In the sky, Pisces is represented as two fish swimming at right angles to each other, one to the north and one to the west. They are attached by a cord. The fish themselves are apparently the Greek goddess Aphrodite and her son, Eros, who turned into fish (space.com).
For a more experienced star gazer, use the information below in finding the constellation:
  • Right Ascension: 0.85 hours
  • Declination: 11.08 degrees
  • Visible: Between latitudes 90 degrees and minus 65 degrees.
