Capricornus Constellation

Best seen in September at 9 p.m., Capricornus is one of the dimmer zodiac constellations, but because of its recognizable shape it's not too difficult to find. One method that is used to find Capricornus starts with finding the ecliptic line. During sunset, start by looking west towards the sunset and finding a bright red dot above the sun, which is Venus. The Moon should be rising in the opposite direction, and from there you trace or imagine a linear path from Venus to the Moon. If two hours after sunset, look east for a little red dot, which is Mars, and trace the linear path from Mars to the Moon. Looking towards the south of the sky, Capricornus is made of bright stars that form a triangle or boomerang shape, and the ecliptic line passes right through it. Another way to find Capricornus is to look for it’s neighbor Sagittarius, which has a recognizable shape of a teapot.
The constellation has its roots in the cultures of Sumeria — which identified it with a mythical figure that was half goat and half fish — and Babylonia, which portrayed it as a goat-human hybrid.(space.com)
For a more experienced star gazer, use the information below in finding the constellation:
  • Right Ascension: 21.02 hours
  • Declination: -20 to -23 degrees
  • Visible: between latitudes 60 degrees and -90 degrees
