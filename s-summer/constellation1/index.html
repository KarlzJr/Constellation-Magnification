Sagittarius Constellation

Best seen August around 9 p.m., Sagittarius is more difficult to find because of the low brightness of its stars and its central location to the Milky Way Galaxy, but because of its signature teapot shape, it can definitely be found with a few methods. In an area with minimal light pollution, use the Milky Way Galaxy to find Sagittarius. The Milky Way has a wider, brighter area and to the left of this area is the constellation. The second method requires finding the ecliptic path, which starts by looking west towards the sunset and finding a bright red dot above the sun, which is Venus. The Moon should be rising in the opposite direction, and from there you trace or imagine a linear path from Venus to the Moon. Look at the ecliptic path until you find Sagittarius and use the tail-end of Scorpius as a place marker for the constellation.
The Greeks identify Sagittarius as a centaur, horse-human hybrid with the body of a man atop the four legs of a horse. The creature is aiming an arrow toward its neighbor Scorpio (space.com).
For a more experienced star gazer, use the information below in finding the constellation:
  • Right ascension: 19.11 hours
  • Declination: -25.8 degrees
  • Visible: between 55 degrees and -90 degrees
