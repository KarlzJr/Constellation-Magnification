Cancer Constellation

Best seen around March at 9 p.m, Cancer is known for being one of the oldest recognized groups of stars. However it is one of the trickiest to spot, so using the Big Dipper and the star Regulus from the Cancer constellation, you will look for two bright stars from the Gemini constellation, Castor and Pollux. In finding Pollux, trace an imaginary line diagonally from the handle of the Big Dipper towards the opposite side of itâ€™s bowl. Continuing that line, you should see Pollux and Castor above it. From there, split the difference between Leo and Gemini and look for a wishbone shaped constellation, with the Altarf star being the brightest. You have now found it!
The constellation of Cancer represents the giant crab that attacked Hercules during the second of the 12 labors he performed as penance for killing his family (space.com).
For a more experienced star gazer, use the information below in finding the constellation:
  • Right ascension: 9 hours
  • Declination: 20 degrees
  • Visible between latitudes 90 degrees and minus 60 degrees
